Bugnauts!

April 2023 - May 2024

Role: Designer

Bugnauts! is a single-player third-person action game that mixes accessible 3D projectile gameplay with environmental education. Players play as Chloe, a young aspiring entomologist who must use her bug net to defeat the giant Phantom Bugs that have overtaken the island she calls home. Bugnauts! is a finished project developed in Unreal Engine 5 as a capstone project at USC.

Contributions

  • Worked cooperatively on a team of 40+ with 2 other designers to create a game for AGP, USC's capstone project class.

  • Greyboxed early forms of levels within the game

  • Prototyped and implemented mechanics and enemy features using Unreal Engine's Blueprint system

  • Created AI for enemy bugs using Unreal Engine's behavior tree systems

  • Created and tweaked the majority of enemy bugs' bullet attack patterns within the game

  • Created design documents for enemies, mechanics, and combat features

Contributions

Early Level Design

Using 2D diagrams for a 3D game

Despite working in a 3D engine to create a 3D game, I and my fellow designers used 2D top-down diagrams to form early versions of our levels. This helped us get a general sense of the space our players would be in and where certain mechanics would be taught. It also allowed us to make better use of the space as we started to greybox and blockout the level in engine.

Behavior Trees

Allowed for quick iterations and changes to Enemy AI

With the help of Behavior Trees I and my fellow designers were able to quickly change and iterate on enemy behavior to create more varied combat challenges for the player. Once we were happy with a particular enemy type's behavior we could make variations of it by switching around the order and positions of nodes on a copy of the tree to make enemy behavior seem more complex than it actually was. It was incredibly useful for helping us decide when a specific attack pattern should be fired at.

Attack Patterns

The creation of enemy attack patterns

This tool, created by one of the engineers on the project allowed designers to create and tweak enemy bullet attack patterns. By adjusting parameters such as bullet speed, direction, spread, and timing, I was able to quickly iterate on attacks that would test the player's movement capabilities. The image above would show me what bullet type was selected, the number of shots in each 'attack' as well as the time between each 'attack'. This pattern was then attached to a node on the behavior tree and ended up being an attack of the Grunt Beetle enemy.

Design Documents

The importance of Documentation

Featured Design Documents

While not the most exciting thing in the world, writing and documenting our ideas was often how a feature got approved for implementation. It was very fun to discuss these with other designers on the team and it helped refine our ideas that were presented on these documents. Additionally, the engineers would not have much to go off of for implementation of a feature without these docs.
