Bugnauts! is a single-player third-person action game about ecosystem restoration. Players control Chloe, a young entomologist who must use her bug net to defeat giant Phantom Bugs that have overtaken her island home. The game mixes accessible 3D projectile gameplay with environmental education and was shipped as a USC Games Capstone project.

Design Goals

The combat needed to be approachable to a broad audience while remaining engaging through encounter variety. Enemy bugs had to feel distinct — each introducing a new movement or attack challenge — without requiring complex AI. The design priority was readable enemy behavior that rewarded player attention and spatial awareness rather than reaction time alone.

Design Deep Dives

Early Level Design

Using 2D diagrams for a 3D game

Despite working in a 3D engine, I and my fellow designers used 2D top-down diagrams for early level planning. This let us quickly explore spatial flow, mechanic placement, and pacing without the cost of building geometry — and gave us a clear reference when starting the greybox in engine.

Enemy Behavior Trees

Rapid AI iteration through Behavior Trees

UE5's Behavior Trees let me and my fellow designers quickly iterate on enemy combat patterns. Once an enemy type's behavior felt right, we could make variations by reordering nodes on a copy of the tree — making enemy behavior feel more complex than the underlying logic actually was. This was particularly valuable for deciding when specific attack patterns should trigger.

Attack Pattern Design

Creating enemy attack patterns from scratch

An engineer on the project built a custom tool that let designers create and tune bullet attack patterns by adjusting parameters — bullet speed, spread, direction, timing, and shot count per burst. I used this tool to rapidly prototype attacks that challenged player movement and spatial awareness.

Each pattern was then attached to a node on the behavior tree. The image above shows a bullet type, shot count, and burst timing for what became the Grunt Beetle's primary attack.

Design Documentation

The importance of documentation on a large team

View Featured Design Documents

On a 40+ person team, documentation was the primary vehicle for getting a feature approved and implemented. Writing a clear design doc forced us to stress-test our ideas before pitching them — and gave engineers a concrete spec to work from. Designing the documents themselves became a design exercise.
