Unchained: Tale of Enra is a tactical RPG that reimagines traditional turn-based combat by introducing a unique Negotiation system. Players can choose to defeat enemies through conventional tactical combat or convince them to lay down their arms by breaking their Resolve — a non-violent approach that offers different rewards and shapes the narrative.

Originally a USC Games Capstone project, Unchained won awards at the 2025 USC Games Expo and continues development beyond the program.

Design Goals

The core goal was to give players genuine tactical agency beyond raw combat power. Each encounter should feel like a puzzle with multiple valid solutions — rewarding players who learn enemy behavior and Resolve thresholds just as much as those who master combat positioning. Enemy AI needed to be readable and consistent so players could form and test strategies without feeling cheated.

Featured Work

Early Development

Designing systems for a turn-based tactics game

Unlike Bugnauts!, Unchained was pitched as a turn-based tactics game. Because of this, a lot of early development time was spent creating and refining systems. I and the other designers worked with paper-prototypes to speed up this process. Once we knew that the systems were fun to interact with, we could then pull the trigger on spending time to create them in engine.

By the time we had a working prototype we had workshopped over 15 different combat systems. Many did not make it into the final game, but key systems — Negotiation, character abilities, Chain abilities, turn order, basic enemy behavior, Line of Sight, and Resolve — all needed to be in place before we could begin testing the core loop with playtesters.

Negotiation System

Convince your foes to lay down their arms or fight with you

The Negotiation system is a core mechanic I designed with help from the rest of the design team. It differentiates Unchained from traditional tactical RPGs by giving players a meaningful non-combat path.

Instead of attacking, players spend their action economy on a turn by Negotiating — selecting pre-made responses to reduce an enemy's Resolve. The Resolve meter is visible on all enemies and affects their behavior: enemies may become debuffed, act more passively, flee, or even join the player's side.

Throughout development I iterated on the system through many prototype versions and UI variants. Extensive early playtesting was essential to refining the pacing and feedback loops.

Enemy AI Design

Player recognition and enemy consistency

For players to form effective strategies, they need a clear model of what enemies are capable of. I designed enemy AI to be intentionally readable and predictable, so players can learn and adapt quickly. Complexity scales through enemy formations rather than individual unit complexity.

The Soldier — first enemy type created, melee unit that attacks the nearest player while also positioning toward weaker targets. Served as the template for all subsequent units.

The Archer — basic ranged enemy that spends a turn aiming before firing. Teaches players to count turns and interrupt or reposition.

The Knight — bulky melee unit that buffs allies before engaging. Players who learn this pattern know to use their tank to taunt the Knight during the buff turn.

Playtesting & User Research

Usability and Player Understanding

Once we had a working prototype I took on the lead usability role. The challenge: not just teaching players our systems through tutorials, but ensuring every piece of UI and game logic communicated enough information for players to make informed tactical decisions mid-encounter.

Over the course of development I conducted 100+ playtests covering individual systems, levels, UI, and overall fun factor. I collected data in a playtest log and presented findings each sprint — surfacing bugs, systemic flaws, and specific iteration opportunities.

As development matured, I used accumulated playtest knowledge to design and implement the game's tutorial sequence, ensuring new players were eased into all systems before their first real encounter.

Level & Conflict Design

The design process for the 1-4 Conflict

After months of systems work, it was time to design levels that put those systems to use. For the 1-4 Conflict I proposed a defense map rooted in the story — the local Neshi population is vulnerable, so players must protect them while fending off waves of enemies.

Defense maps can become static without evolving objectives. I addressed this by adding a mid-map twist: enemies blow open a wall at the south, creating a third entry point just as players think they have the north covered. This forces real-time adaptation with limited tanking resources.
