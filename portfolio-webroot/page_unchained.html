Unchained: Tale of Enra

August 2024 - Present

Role: Designer

Unchained: Tale of Enra is a tactical RPG that reimagines traditional turn-based combat by introducing a unique 'Negotiation' system. Players can choose to either defeat their enemies using traditional tactical combat AND/OR by convincing them to lay down their arms via breaking their Resolve with non-violent means. Each approach offers different rewards and different methods of success, impacting the protagonists' development and choices in the narrative.

This game was originally created as a Capstone project in the USC Games program using Unity, and has since expanded beyond that to further explore its potential after winning awards at the 2025 USC Games Expo. It is currently still in development.

Early Development

Designing systems for a turn-based tactics game

Unlike Bugnauts!, Unchained was pitched as a turn-based tactics game. Because of this, a lot of early development time was spent creating and refining systems. I and the other designers worked with paper-prototypes to speed up this process. Once we knew that the systems were fun to interact with, we could then pull the trigger on spending time to create them in the game engine.

By the time we had a working prototype we had workshopped over 15 different combat systems. Many of them did not make it in to the final game, but many of them were essential to the game's identity and were needed before the prototype was complete. Systems like Negotiation, Character abilities and Chain abilities, turn order, basic enemy behavior, Line of Sight, and Resolve all needed to be in place before we could start testing the core combat loop with playtesters.

Negotiation System

Convince your foes to lay down their arms or fight with you

The Negotiation system is a core mechanic designed by me with help from the rest of design that differentiates Unchained from traditional tactical RPGs. Players can choose to either defeat enemies in combat or convince them to lay down their arms, offering multiple approaches to each encounter.

Instead of using their offensive abilities, players can spend their action-economy on a turn by Negotiating. In this system, players select pre-made responses as they converse with enemies in an attempt to reduce their Resolve. The Resolve meter is present on all enemies throughout a level and affects their behavior with player characters depending on its value. Enemies may become debuffed, act more passively, flee, or even join the player's side if the right conditions are met.

Throughout development, I worked with designers to iterate on the system. It led to many prototypes and different versions of the UI. Extensive playtesting in early development was essential in refining the system.

Enemy AI

Player recognition and enemy consistency

In order for players to come up with effective strategies, they need to have an understanding of what their enemies are capable of. I designed the enemies and their AI to be relatively simple and act more predictably so players can quickly learn and adapt to their behavior. I created a variety of enemy types to accomodate the simplicity of their behaviors. This way we can up the complexity of enemy formations to scale difficulty rather than spending more time designing a complex enemy unit. Featured Design Documents

This soldier unit was the first enemy type that was created and served as a base for the rest to come. This melee unit was designed to attack the nearest player unit while also positioning themselves closer to other player units who may be less bulky.

The archer was designed as a basic ranged enemy. This unit takes a turn off to aim their shot, and then fires if there's a player unit in range.

The knight is a bulky melee unit that can buff its allies. This unit takes a turn to activate its buff, and then acts similar to the soldier. If the player can figure out this pattern, they will learn to use their tank to stop the knight from buffing by taunting them.

Usability

Usability and Player Understanding

Once we had finished a version of our prototype, because I was already familiar with the game's design I took on the challenge of testing for the game's Usability. Not only must Players be given effective tutorials for our systems, but the systems and UI must also communicate information to the player to allow them to make an informed decision during the Conflicts (levels).

Over the course of development, I conducted over a hundred playtests to test the individual systems, levels, UI, and overall fun factor. I collected and recorded the data from them in a playtest log and then presented the data to the rest of the team to point out bugs, flaws in the systems, and things we could iterate on to make the game better.

As the team got further into development, and we knew what player's struggled with and what content was going to make it in, I used my knowledge from playtests to put together a tutorial for the game.

Level and Conflict Design

Player agency and meaningful choices

We designed Unchained around the philosophy of giving players agency and meaningful choices. Every encounter can be approached in multiple ways, and the story responds to the player's decisions, rewarding both combat mastery and negotiation skill.
